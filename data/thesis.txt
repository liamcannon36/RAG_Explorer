ABSTRACT
People in the United States living with intellectual and developmental disabil-
ities (I/DD) often experience trauma at a disproportionate rate. Due to societal
and personal barriers, individuals with I/DD have problems with gaining access
to therapy, thus often having to live with the negative effects of their untreated
trauma. The effects of untreated trauma can cause severe impairment which affects
a person’s quality of life and emotional, social, and physical development.
Post-trauma self-regulation is a way of doing activities to regulate one’s emo-
tions to help cope with the negative effects of trauma. It has been shown as an
effective method of helping individuals with I/DD momentarily cope with their
trauma, and has been used complementary to therapy. It has also been shown
that individuals with I/DD are avid users of mobile computing devices. To this
end, we present the prototyping and design of Bodhi, a post-trauma self-regulation
mobile computing app for people with I/DD.
We first create a initial prototype, based on prior research we have done with
the I/DD community. To evaluate this initial prototype, we did a user study with
lay members of the I/DD community, recruiting (5) people with I/DD. These par-
ticipants represent the eventual users of the app. After gathering their feedback,
we found that while participants enjoyed the prototype, the self-care activities in
the prototype needed to be fleshed out, and other features reworked. We analyzed
the findings and incorporated these ideas when creating Bodhi, a fully-functioning
mobile computing app. We did another user study, again with five (5) new individ-
uals with I/DD, and analyzed the findings. We analyzed this feedback, and found
that participants largely enjoyed Bodhi and would use it for self-regulation. Lastly,
we discuss the implications of our research and identify future areas of research
that should be explored.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Trauma is a word derived from the Greek word “traˆuma”, that directly trans-
lates to a “wound” or “penetration” that leaves a lasting impact [1]. Specifically,
trauma is defined as “a sudden, potentially deadly experience, often leaving lasting,
troubling memories” [1]. In the United States, it is suspected that a significant
proportion of individuals with intellectual and developmental disabilities
(I/DD) have experienced trauma in the past [2]. I/DD is defined by the American
Association of Intellectual and Developmental Disabilities as such: “A condition
characterized by significant limitations in both intellectual functioning and adaptive
behavior that originates before the age of 22 ” [3]. Furthermore, it can be charac-
terized as a range of disabilities which negatively impact a person’s intellectual,
emotional, and/or physical development [3, 4].
Trauma is highly prevalent in the I/DD community for a few reasons, which we
have explored in our previous work [5]. We identify three major causes of trauma in
this community. First, there is rampant abuse among people with I/DD. Supported
by FBI statistics gathered in 2017, it was shown that sexual abuse occurs seven
times as often as non-disabled folks [6], and violent victimization occurs four times
as often as non-disabled folks [7]. Of those who report abuse, 90% have been abused
multiple times, and 50% disclose being abused more times than they can count.
Second, people with I/DD experience voicelessness, that is, people with I/DD’s
opinions and voices are often discounted and not taken into consideration [8]. This
negation of their reality, opinions and experiences, is another source of trauma
[9]. Third, people with I/DD often live highly managed lives; they may have less
control of their lives, schedule, and own financial resources [10]. Thus, they often
1
lack control over what they do with their own time and their own environment,
which is another source of trauma [4]. Thus, we strongly believe any technology
built for individuals with I/DD should be trauma-informed
The negative effects of trauma for individuals with I/DD can be crippling
[11]. These symptoms include: (1) an impaired working memory in their daily life;
(2) negative mental health conditions like extreme stress, anxiety, and depression;
(3) excessive distraction, which may impede a person’s ability to function daily;
(4) increased vulnerability to triggering1 events, due to less control over their
environment(for example, group living homes and not being in charge of their own
schedule) [4].
Since people with I/DD often do not get the appropriate care for
their past trauma, many have to live with the effects of their untreated
trauma. Untreated trauma can have long-lasting effects and cause severe impair-
ment, that can affect a person’s general, social, emotional, and physical well-being
[13]. Recent therapeutic treatments for trauma focus on processing the traumatic
event the person experienced [11]. Some of these treatments have shown promise
with the I/DD community, namely cognitive behavioral therapy, and eye move-
ment desensitization and reprocessing [11]. Although these therapeutic methods
have been shown to be effective for people with I/DD [11], there is both societal
and personal barriers which prevent individuals with I/DD from receiving these
therapeutic services. Some societal barriers include: (1) diagnostic overshadow-
ing, where the negative aspects of a person’s trauma are dismissed as being part
of their disability; (2) a lack of healthcare practitioners who are properly equipped
or willing to work with individuals with I/DD [14]. Personal barriers that people
with I/DD may face are: (1) a lack of transportation methods to receive in-person
care; (2) a lack of knowledge about mental health and stigmas which discourage

2
them from getting appropriate care [14]. Because of these barriers, a majority of
people with I/DD may never get appropriate care for their prior trauma, which
may reduce their quality of life [15]. Furthermore, these difficulties in receiving
appropriate care for the effects of their trauma can constitute a traumatic event
in itself, as people with I/DD are further misunderstood and neglected [9, 16].
Self-regulation, which is the act of coping with the negative effects of trauma
by oneself, has been shown as an effective tool for individuals with I/DD to help
momentarily cope with trauma, when used complementary to therapy [11]. In prac-
tice, self-regulation involves performing activities which focuses one’s attention to
cope with the current negative feelings they are feeling [17]. Thus, empower-
ing individuals with I/DD by giving them greater means to perform
self-regulation can help them cope with their trauma in the moment.
Once again, we wish to reiterate that self-regulation is not a replacement for ther-
apy, yet works complementary to therapy. Specifically, we focus on self-regulation
in the context of having experienced prior trauma. Thus, we define post-trauma
self-regulation (PTSR) as such: “Engaging in one or more activities that regulate
one’s emotions to help cope with the state of dis-ease in the moment, outside of
(but complementary to) any long-term therapy” [18].
In recent years, mobile computing devices have become exponentially more
involved in daily life, such as smartphones and tablets. Although there have been
mobile computing applications made for similar purposes, we found there to be
no PTSR app that exists that is suitable for use by individuals with
I/DD (see Chapter 2 for related work in this area). We have found in our prior
work that individuals with I/DD use a host of ersatz (inferior substitute for) apps
for post-trauma self-regulation, although none of these apps is suitable for people
with I/DD [4]. Although there has been some societal stigmas that people with
3
I/DD cannot use devices to the full capacity as non-disabled people, recent research
has pointed to the contrary - individuals with I/DD are adept and avid users of
mobile computing devices [19, 20]. In 2016, it was reported that of the partici-
pants with cognitive disabilities, 72% use smartphones, and 50% use tablets. In
particular, individuals with I/DD may favor tablets, for their larger, often simpler
user interface and for increased gesture recognition than smartphones [21] There-
fore, building a mobile computing app is one of the most direct ways to
provide individuals with I/DD a greater ability to perform PTSR.
1.1 Building for and recruiting individuals with I/DD who have expe-
rienced trauma
We focus on the subset of the I/DD community who has experienced trauma
because it is suspected that a high proportion of people with I/DD have experi-
enced it [13]. In this sense, we use the I/DD term broadly, as in [22]. This is
because we are focusing on the person’s abilities, rather than focusing on their
medical diagnosis which may or may not reflect their experiences with their dis-
ability.
Throughout the project, we plan to work with adults with I/DD, both with
experts and lay members of the community alike. Typically, it would be appro-
priate to recruit and gain feedback from the population in which the app will be
designed for. Thus, it would seem to follow that the appropriate population to
recruit from would be members of the I/DD community who have previously ex-
perienced trauma. Based on our approach to recruitment in our prior research
with this community [23], and advice from the stakeholders whom we have worked
with, we have found that this strategy can be problematic.
We have found that the most ethical form of recruitment in this case is
to recruit from the general population of individuals with I/DD, and not
4
those who have disclosed that they have previously been traumatized.
The reasoning behind this is twofold: (1) if we want to recruit only the members of
the I/DD community who have been traumatized, it would be necessary to ask the
individuals about the past trauma in order to determine if they meet the criteria
for the study. This act of asking about the previous trauma has the real potential
to re-traumatize them, by unexpectedly bringing back traumatizing memories; (2)
with the high likelihood of an individual with I/DD having experienced trauma,
it means that the participants may have experienced past trauma, even without
disclosing it.
1.2 A description of the research process
Here, we present the prototyping and design of Bodhi, a post-trauma self-
regulation mobile computing app for adults with I/DD. Bodhi is primarily made
up of six (6) self-regulation activities. These activities took large inspiration from
prior work we have done with the community [4, 23], as we will describe in Chapter
3. It is important to note that Bodhi serves not to replace the need for therapeutic
treatment, but to complement it.
This research is a continuation of the work reported in the ASSETS ’22 paper
[4]. This paper discusses and puts forth guidelines for designing post-trauma self-
regulation apps, that are grounded in the work of trauma-informed care [4]. It
also follows the recommendations specified in [5]. These are actionable steps for
designers to follow when building for the I/DD community, and we adhered to
these steps throughout the design of Bodhi.

We first did a survey of the surrounding research, as described in Chapter 2.
We found that there were no PTSR apps that were suitable for individuals with

We then built the initial prototype, from which we took inspiration from prior
work we have done with the I/DD community, as described in Chapter 3. In
our first user study, we interviewed five (5) participants, all with varying forms of
I/DD, using semi-structured interviews after they used the prototype for a short
period of time (as described in Chapter 4). This study was conducted over Zoom,
with participants using the remote control feature to interact with the prototype.
We then analyzed this feedback using reflexive thematic analysis, as described in
Chapter 5. Overall, we found three major points of feedback: (1) the self-regulation
activities were widely enjoyed, although they needed to be further fleshed out;
6
(2) the avatar was well-received, although it needed greater customizability; (3)
participants were optimistic about using the app, however the engagement elements
had to be reworked.
We analyzed the feedback and assessed the strengths and weaknesses of the
current prototype. Using this, we developed Bodhi, a fully functioning mobile-
computing-based app, as described in Chapter 6. Then to evaluate Bodhi, we did
a second user study, again using the semi-structured interviews format with five
(5) new participants with varying forms of I/DD. Again, this study was conducted
over Zoom, with participants using the remote control feature to use the app.
The details of the methodology used for this study are described in Chapter 7.
Totaling both of the user studies, we included ten (10) participants, all with some
form of I/DD. We then analyzed the findings of the second user study, again using
reflexive thematic analysis as described in Chapter 8. Compared to the initial
prototype, Bodhi retained the core activities and features, but improved upon the
prototype’s weaknesses. We found three major findings: (1) most of the activities
were well received by participants; (2) the avatar was appreciated in the app, and
was helpful to participants; (3) most participants would use Bodhi frequently, and
the engagement elements were effective in motivating the user. They were typically
favorable of the self-regulation activities in the app, and largely differed in which
activities they enjoyed the most.
Based off our findings in the user study evaluating Bodhi, we provide the
following points of discussion in Chapter 9: (1) The self-regulation activities in
Bodhi may need to be more explicit in their functionality; (2) The descriptive
ability of the self-regulation activities in Bodhi may need to improve.
Finally, we conclude the research by reiterating what has been accomplished
in this project. Then, we list some future areas of work and research, as described
7
in Chapter 10. We provide the following recommendations to improve Bodhi,
and to help other designers create post-trauma self-regulation apps for the I/DD
community: (1) the avatar’s conversational ability should be explored further needs
to be explored further, and has potential; (2) user authentication and security in
Bodhi needs to be explored further; (3) a recommendation system for the activities
in the app needs to be explored further; (4) the socialization aspect of Bodhi should
be explored further.

CHAPTER 2
Related work
In this section, we will describe the current state of the research around post-
trauma self-regulation (PTSR) mobile computing apps. In general, we found there
to be no PTSR apps available for individuals with I/DD . Below, we split
the prior work done into three categories, which we will describe below.
2.1 Mobile apps for mental health
Over the past decade, there has been a myriad of apps released which focus
on mental health. These include but are not limited to: clinical-focused apps,
which serve to diagnose a patient or connect them to a specialist [1], artificial
intelligence chatbot apps, which simulate human conversation [2, 3], and targeted
mental health apps, such as those that deal with depression or anxiety [4, 5].
These apps provide different forms of psychotherapy, such as cognitive behavioral
therapy or exposure therapy [6, 7]. There is also an increasing number of apps
which focus on self-management, which enable users to care for their own symptoms
[8, 9]. Although some of these apps hold promise for providing/supporting effective
mental health care, the efficacy of these apps are in question, and the vast majority
of them remain unstudied [10, 11, 12]. Furthermore, these apps are inapplicable
to our work for two reasons: (1): most of the apps are based on providing or
supporting therapy, which differs from self-regulation. (2): these apps are not
designed with the I/DD community as the target population.
2.2 Mobile apps for non-trauma-based self-regulation
There now exists a variety of mobile-computing apps that provide self-
regulation, but there is again a glaring lack of research on their efficacy [13].
11
This includes emotion tracking apps, such as “Emotion map”, that focuses on
logging emotions, where a user can log the date location, and anonymously share
them if they wish [14]. Most commonly, these self-regulation apps, such as Calm
and Headspace, have a strong focus on guided meditation. Of the popular self-
regulation apps commercially available however, most pose problems for the I/DD
community.
Although individuals with I/DD may use these apps, they are not well-suited
for this community for a host of reasons. Many of the activities in these guided
meditation apps have characteristics which have the potential to trigger trauma
survivors, which make up a large proportion of the I/DD community. For instance,
an activity which asks the user to close their eyes in order to meditate can trigger a
trauma survivor [15]. In addition, many of these apps require a high cognitive load
for the user, due to the overabundance of choices in the app, with little attention
paid on how to present this well to the user. One example of this is Insight Timer,
which supplies hundreds of thousands of videos to the users [16]. Paired with very
limited guidance or instructions, and limited recommendations of what content
to look at, users of our population may struggle to use them as it is common to
have a weaker working memory. On top of that, many of these apps have hefty
subscription fees. For instance, Calm [17], Headspace [18], and Breethe [19] all
hover around 15 USD a month to use, which greatly reduces accessibility to a
community with increased financial challenges.
The style of the guided meditations in these apps can also pose problems.
These meditations are guided by vocal cues, but are rather abstract and are bet-
ter suited toward those experienced in practicing meditation. In addition to the
unfamiliarity with the concept, the pace of the instructions also commonly poses
a problem [20]. To summarize, these apps are designed for people with the means
12
to purchase a subscription, who have not experienced prior trauma, and who have
a strong working memory and ability to hold abstract concepts. It follows then
that these apps pose serious constraints that may prevent individuals from I/DD
from using them.
2.3 Mobile apps for trauma-focused self-regulation
There is a small class of mobile computing apps which provide self-regulation
in the context of trauma. The US government has developed a class of apps for
military members and families to cope with their prior trauma. These apps vary in
their function: some apps teach mindfulness practices [21], provide means to cope
with prior trauma [22], and some help the families of trauma survivors [23]. These
apps are useful in that they do much better in considering trauma in their design
than the aforementioned apps. This does not solve the root problem however,
that these apps are often inaccessible to individuals with I/DD. Most of these
apps are extremely reading-heavy, which is ideal for helping users understand and
recognize trauma, but is inaccessible to the I/DD community where limited literacy
is commonplace [24]. This, in addition to poor recommendations (as discussed
before), makes these apps extremely difficult for individuals with I/DD to navigate
and use.
2.4 Ersatz apps which people with I/DD currently utilize for post-
trauma self-regulation (PTSR)
Our prior research which studied the examined the use of PTSR apps by
people with I/DD has determined that they use a variety of ersatz apps for PTSR
[15]. That is, people with I/DD use apps which were not designed for post-trauma
self-regulation, but serve as a substitute due to the lack of suitable apps currently
available. In a study with practitioners, many of whom worked daily with people
with I/DD for years, reported that people with I/DD were using, but not limited
13
to: (1) audio-visual apps (e.g, Youtube, Spotify, video games); (2) social media
apps (e.g, Instagram, Facebook); (3) creativity apps (coloring apps, creating music
playlist apps), (4) apps with a digital companion (e.g, CareCoach) [15]. The
practitioners interviews aligned with our findings, that these apps are not well-
suited for people with I/DD [15]. Thus, while people with I/DD may use substitute
apps, there are no suitable PTSR apps for people with I/DD.

CHAPTER 3
The initial prototype description
We will now describe the initial prototype that we created, based on our prior
research we have done with the I/DD community [1, 2]. Next, we will describe
each major screen of the prototype below.
3.1 Inspiration for the self-regulation activities in the prototype
The self-regulation activities in the prototype take inspiration from peer-
support meetings held by local I/DD self-advocacy agencies we have worked with.
These are organizations where self-advocates with I/DD advocate for their rights
and needs, which are sorely underrepresented in the public sphere. In peer-support
meetings, self-advocates, who receive training in conducting self-regulation sessions
work with a person with I/DD through a series of self-regulation activities. This
gives the peer a space to do self-regulation and to have someone to talk to that may
better understand their disability. Due to conducting these peer-support meetings
and working with members of the community, self-advocates tend to be experts
and very knowledgeable about the community’s wants and needs.
The self-regulation activities in our prototype take inspiration from these peer-
support meetings, and suggestions from self-advocates that we have gathered in
our prior research [1, 2]. Of these, we chose the activities which we thought were
most able to be translated to an app. Next, we will describe the activities that we
chose for the prototype.
3.2 A description of the self-regulation activities in the prototype
The bulk of the prototype is made up of six self-regulation activities that
users can choose from, as seen in Figure 2. It is to note that in the prototype, the
17
self-regulation activities are named “self-care activities”. This is a more familiar
and colloquial term for users.
Figure 2. The self-regulation activities menu in the prototype. This represents our
choice of activities that we want participants to interact with and give feedback
on.
Each self-regulation activity is set up as a virtual room, with the user interface
and buttons making use of skeuomorphism. This is a design concept in which
objects in a digital interface closely resemble their physical counterparts [3]. This
concept is diametrically opposed to flat design, a modern-appearing style in which
minimalism, simplicity, and two-dimensional objects are preferred [3]. Despite
this, there is evidence that some marginalized populations (in this case, the elderly
population which apps are seldomly designed for) may suffer from this large-scale
transfer to flat design by many products, and struggle to use flat design products
as efficiently [4]. We believe that skeuomorphic design may prove beneficial for the
I/DD population, as flat icon design requires understanding abstract concepts and
impose a higher cognitive load on users [5]. Thus, the elements in this prototype
18
closely resemble their physical counterparts, often opting for a retro feel. We feel
this may provide a more immersive and comfortable experience for individuals with
I/DD.
Along with almost every screen in the prototype, there is an avatar. The
avatar guides the user with text in the speech bubble, which briefly instructs the
user on how to start using the activity. This avatar is meant to serve as a personal
guide, to prevent the user from getting lost or confused, as the activities can be
open-ended. Prior work which has examined the use of avatars in crowdsourcing
engagement has found them to be effective motivators to keep performing a task,
and help reduce cognitive load on the user [6, 7]. In particular, they found that the
conversational feedback with worker avatars made crowdworker participants feel
more positively about their work, and that their tasks were less difficult [7]. For
similar reasons, we believe the avatar could help engage the users of our eventual
app with I/DD, by increasing motivation to do self-regulation activities and by
aiding them in feeling like the self-regulation activities are more doable.
The user is free to enter whichever activity they choose. In addition, these
activities can be used at the speed of the user - that is, the user is free to use the
activity as long as they wish, and there is no required step or action they have
to take to “complete” an activity. After they finish using an activity, the user is
brought to a reward screen, then back to the self-regulation activities menu. This
flow is shown in Figure 3.
19
Figure 3. The flow of the self-regulation activities in the prototype. First, the user
selects a self-regulation activity from the menu. Then, they enter the activity and
interact with it as long as they wish. Then, they are brought to the reward screen,

In some of the activities, the user first enters a selection screen where they
can choose the type of content they would like to see or interact with. After doing
this, they can go on to do one of the activities, which range widely in terms of
how interactive they are. Below, we will describe each activity in brief:
• The coloring activity:
In the selection screen, the user has the option to select one drawing from a
number of pre-made drawings that they would like to color in. Once inside
the activity, the user can color the drawing whatever way they choose, as
depicted in Figure 4. They also have the option to save the drawing to the
gallery, or save a draft of it to return to later.

Figure 4. The coloring activity where users can select a colored pencil and do

• The positive thoughts activity:
The user is given the option of multiple categories to choose from, with each
category containing a certain type of positive quotes. In the prototype, there
were four categories to choose from: Loving yourself, Being positive and
happy, Not giving up, and Noticing the world around you. After selecting a
category, the user is shown positive thoughts which pertain to that category,
that they can cycle through at their own pace. Each positive thought is
accompanied by a background of a nature scene, as shown in Figure 5.

Figure 5. The positive thoughts activity, where users see a positive affirmation,

• The music activity:
The user can choose from the songs or soundscapes categories. Songs are

a neurodivergent artist who was willing to create original content for our
prototype (and eventual app). She gave us a ”self-care song” and a ”self-care
soundscape”, which we made available in this activity. Including I/DD artists
for the music in this prototype (and the eventual app) served two purposes:
(1) to promote the creations of I/DD artists and content creators, and to
increase the visibility of the community; (2) to foster a sense of community
and inclusivity among our users, and to make users feel less alone.

Figure 6. The music activity, where users can select a song or a soundscape made

Here, the user can select a category of videos to watch, and cycle through

from the I/DD community, to foster a sense of community and to imrpove
visibility of I/DD content creators.
23
Figure 7. The video activity, where users can watch different types of videos.
• The arcade activity:
Figure 8 shows the arcade activity, which contains a tapping activity. Here,
the user is given a random shape and has to tap all the matching shapes.
The user can take as much time as they want, and are not penalized for

3.3 The avatar and narration
Traditionally, a screen reader has often been the only option for the user to
have the content on the screen read out to them. Through prior research however
[8], we determined that narration can be a very powerful tool to aid people with
I/DD in using a mobile app. To provide narration in an app means having audio
that describes the contents of a given screen, but in a more comprehensive way
than a screen reader would [8]. Instead of just reading the contents of an object
when prompted, narration describes the screen in more detail, and in a voice that
gives a more conversational feeling. In essence, we have built a talking app, which
is divergent from the majority of self-regulation apps which are quiet. This serves
to reduce the high cognitive load that reading can impose on our population.
In addition, this option provides some relief for those who have major difficulty
reading, or are unable to read at all, and gives an alternate route for them to
27
consume the contents of the app the app.
In the prototype, almost all screens include narration. In our case, each screen
has some brief instructions which lightly guide the user in order to get them started
with the activity or screen. The voice of the avatar speaks the instructions in a
conversational and inviting tone, yet at a slow and clear pace so that the instruc-
tions can be easily understood. In addition to the instructions, the narration also
extends to any screen with text in the speech bubble. For instance, the avatar
narrates the positive thought that the user is interacting with, and the avatar also
narrates the acknowledgment screen which appears after a user interacts with an
activity. Lastly, if the user wishes to not have the narration, they can disable the
avatar’s voice in settings.
3.4 Other features in the prototype
There were some other features in the prototype that users had the option
to see, primarily the trusted person feature. A remnant of our research group’s
previous app, “R3: Recognize, Report, Respond”, this feature lets the user set the
name and phone number of someone they deeply trust and can talk to in times
of distress [9]. This is an important feature for a self-regulation app, as the user
may become overwhelmed or just simply have the urge to talk to someone they
can confide in.
Lastly, the prototype has a reward system, where users are given a reward as
positive feedback for using any of the self-regulation activities. This is accompanied
by an applause sound when they receive the reward.

Methodology of the user study with the prototype
We will now describe how we conducted the first user study, including recruit-
ment, the means of running the study, and how we collected results.
4.1 Study goals and format
The main goal of this user study was primarily to gather information about
what they thought about the self-regulation activities, the interactive elements
of the app, and whether they would use it. In addition, we gathered feedback
about what participants would want in the eventual app. To do this, we used a
semi-structured interview format, which is ideal for gaining valuable qualitative
information. As opposed to a structured interview, the semi-structured format
allows for more follow-up and exploration of topics. It is more restricted than an
unstructured interview however, which is most similar to a free-flowing conversa-
tion. This gave us a balance between gathering good depth of answers, while still
gaining information about topics we were interested in. The full list of questions
can be found in Appendix A.
4.2 Study recruitment and details
After creating the questions and format for the user study, we first received
approval from the Institutional Review Board (IRB) for the study (IRB Number:
2079954). Then, we carried out the user study using the initial prototype of the
Bodhi app, with individuals living with I/DD. These participants are members of
the I/DD community but are not experts in this domain, thus representing the
future users of Bodhi. There were five (5) participants recruited for this study,
all with varying types of I/DD (Table 1). All participants were given an informed
31
consent form to sign, a guideline to the form, and a consent checklist. These
forms put the concept of consent in plain terms, and ensure that the participant
is made clearly aware of the terms of the study. The informed consent made the
participants aware of the fact that the session was recorded, that their personal
information will not be identifiable, and that they can pause or stop the user study
at any time they wish. We also made it clear to participants that they could reach
out to us with any questions or concerns as well. The user study took place over
the course of three weeks, and each participant used the prototype separately from
one another (and was interviewed separately). The sessions took place remotely
over Zoom, where participants used the remote control feature to use the prototype
on our screen.
Participants used the prototype for a short period of time, about 15-20 min-
utes, before giving an interview. Zoom was chosen for conducting the studies, in
order to increase the chances of recruitment and to reduce logistical difficulties
for our participants. Prior work has evaluated the validity of remote usability
testing in the context of synchronous remote testing, such as using a prototype
[1, 2]. It has been shown that remote testing can expedite recruitment, and is of-
ten preferred by participants [1, 2]. It is to note that there is a risk of unexpected
technical difficulties with remote conferencing technology, and that there could be
more information gathered by being present in the environment in which the user
would likely use the app [2]. However, this has to be considered in the context of
the I/DD community. Since people with I/DD may have lower energy to spend,
less control over their schedule, and transportation issues [3], it is understandable
that meeting in person is much more of a burden than joining a Zoom session.
This greatly reduces recruitment, which is already a sparse domain. We believe
that although there may be context missed by doing a remote study, this cost is
32
trumped by the greatly increased convenience and accessibility that remote studies
provide. In this study, all participants joined the Zoom session on a laptop or PC.

4.3 Limitations
This study has a couple of limitations. First, the user study was conducted
remotely over Zoom. While it did increase the number of participants and make it
easier for many to attend the meeting, it may have reduced the number of observa-
tions we were able to make. There may have been visual cues and body language
that was hard to pick up on in the Zoom that would have been more noticeable
had we done an in-person study. Second, the participants in this study ranged
from 25-31 years old, which does not represent the entire span of ages of potential
users of our app (any adults with I/DD). A more diverse set of participants could
perhaps have given us additional insights into our prototype. That being said,
none of these limitations affected the outcomes of this study.
4.4 Study analysis
After the study, we transcribed the Zoom recordings. The transcriptions were
performed by the author of this paper, who has a background in accessibility
and human-computer interaction research. Then, we performed a reflexive the-
matic analysis on on the transcripts. We used Braun and Clark’s 6-step recursive
approach to reflexive thematic analysis, whose process is described in [4]. Re-
flexive thematic analysis is a qualitative research method which emphasizes the
researcher’s own experiences and biases, and how these play a role in analyzing
the data.
The codes and themes were produced inductively, and changed throughout the
thematic analysis. This method allows for a deeper understanding of the partici-
pants’ responses, as we do not attempt to impose our predefined themes onto their
34
responses. First, we read through the transcripts in their entirety, a fundamental
step to gaining familiarity with the data [4]. Then, we generated initial codes,
which roughly grouped data points into broad categories (ex: comments about the
avatar). These codes are listed below:
• Self-regulation activities that participants say they do: This code is comprised
of activities that the participants say outright that they do. These can be
in-person activities, those done on computing devices, but we are focused on
the actual activity being done, not the delivery of the activity. Ex: We are
not focused on Youtube, Care Coach, etc...
• Comments/criticisms on our prototype’s self-regulation activities: This code
contains the info the participants told us about our activities. This code is
only covering information about the MEDIUM, not the content.
• Comments/criticisms on the content in our prototype’s activities: This code
contains what the participants said about the content in our self-regulation
activities. They are separate from the activities, in that it is concerning the
actual assets, not the design and feel of the activity.
• Comments/criticisms on the prototype’s avatar: This code contains the par-
ticipants’ thoughts about the avatar. This includes what screens it’s in, the
look of the avatar, its role, and comments about the voice of the avatar.
• Comments/criticisms on the prototype’s user interface: This code contains
the participant’s thoughts about the look and feel of the prototype. This
includes color, design choices, layout of screens, and navigation of the proto-
type.
• Comments/criticisms on the prototype’s instructions: This code contains
35
the participant’s thoughts about the speech bubble instructions in the proto-
type. This can include comments about the wording, clarity of instructions,
need for instructions, and other thoughts about the instructions. Also, this
contains info about the participants’ ideas on how to interact with these in-
structions (read, listen, etc...). Lastly, any comments about how the eventual
app should respond to the user, or things the user would want to say to the
app come here.
• Ideas for our eventual app that the participants proposed: This code contains
new ideas that the participants had for our app. This can be ideas for new
activities, or ideas to enhance the activities with new things.
• Comments/criticisms about the rewards: This code contains the participants’
thoughts about the rewards they saw. This includes how often they get
rewards, what the rewards are, etc. . .
• Comments/criticisms about the non-activity screens: This code contains
ideas, comments, or criticisms the participants had about screens that were
not captured by any of the above codes, such as comments about the trusted
person screen.
• Self-regulation activities (not in the prototype) that participants mention:
This code contains info about self-regulation activities participants mention.
This does not have to be activities they do, but just activities that they
mention.
• Comments/criticisms about the prototype in general: This code contains info
about the prototype in the broader sense. These include answers about who
they would recommend the app to, how they felt using it, and broader picture
feelings about the prototype.
36
From these codes, we generated themes, which further categorize the data in a
code. From there, we could analyze the data, understanding the most salient
points of each theme and gathering interesting points.
The results of this analysis will be explained in the next section.

CHAPTER 6
The Bodhi app: A description
After conducting the first user study, we gained a sense of what users enjoyed
about the prototype, and what aspects could be improved. Below, we will list the
main areas of improvement in which Bodhi expands upon the initial prototype.
• The self-regulation activities were well-received, and were good
options to be developed further
As discussed, participants differed in what their favorite self-regulation ac-
tivities in the prototype were. Although the feedback was largely positive,
it was clear that the feedback was limited by the rudimentary nature of the
activities in their current state. For instance, some participants were not in
favor of the guided meditation video which blurred their perception of the
video activity, since that was only one of two videos present. We will describe
in the next section how each of these activities were fleshed out.
The selection screens, where users could select the content before entering
an activity, where universally received well. Thus, this was extended so that
each activity has a selection screen before it, and that they are standardized.
Now, before the user interacts with any activity, they first select the content
they wish to see. For example, the user selects the category of music they
would like to hear before going into that activity.
Participants also made it clear that they would want to share the features
in the app to others, namely their trusted person but also to friends and
family. To allow for the user to share their items to whomever they please,
we did two changes: (1) we made it so that the user can share many different
52
items to the gallery (for example, a positive thought or a reward) instead
of just a drawing; (2) we created the sharing option, which allows users to
share via text, via another app, or even create a Apple Watch face of an
item in their gallery.
• Participants were interested in greater customization of the avatar:
It was evident that the participants wanted the avatar to be more customiz-
able, and to have more options for the avatar. For instance, one participant
wanted the avatar to resemble them, while another participant wanted the
avatar to be clip-art, and have a gender-neutral appearance. To achieve
this, we added the “Change my buddy” screen. Here, the user can change
their avatar option and give it a customized name if they wish. The avatar
options encompass a broad array of different types of avatars, ranging from
people, to animals, to anthropomorphic objects. We will describe this screen
more thoroughly in the “Avatar and narration” section.
• The reward system needed to be modified in order to not exhaust
the rewards so quickly:
The rewards were generally enjoyed by participants, however for those who
did not care for the collectible rewards, it quickly grew tiresome. In addition,
it became clear in the study that if a user was going through activities very
rapidly for months at a time, they would quickly run out of rewards and they
would begin getting repeat rewards.
To counteract these problems, we created the acknowledgement screen, which
will be depicted in the “Reward and acknowledgement screens” section. In
53
short, this screen appears after every activity instead of the reward screen.
This screen contains a crown image and a positive message of encouragement.
Instead of showing up every time, the reward screen now shows up occasion-
ally, at random intervals between five and ten activities done. This serves to
keep the rewards engaging and unpredictable, while still acknowledging that
the user did self-regulation. In addition, it exhausts the reward collection
much more slowly.

gives a more conversational feeling. In addition, this option provides some relief
for those who have major difficulty reading, or are unable to read at all, and gives
an alternate route for them to understand the app.

We used a semi-structured interview format for our study questions, which
is ideal for gaining valuable qualitative information. As opposed to a structured
interview, the semi-structured format allows for more follow-up and exploration
of topics. It is more restricted than an unstructured interview however, which
is most similar to a free flowing conversation. This gave us a balance between
gathering good depth of answers, while still gaining information about topics we
were interested in. The full list of questions can be found in Appendix B.

7.4 Study analysis
After the study, we transcribed the Zoom recordings. The transcriptions were
performed by the author of this paper, who has a background in accessibility and
human-computer interaction research. Then, we performed a reflexive thematic
analysis on on the transcripts, in the same manner in which we did in the first user
study.
We used Braun and Clark’s 6-step recursive approach to reflexive thematic
analysis, whose process is described in [2]. Reflexive thematic analysis is a qual-
itative research method which emphasizes the researcher’s own experiences and
biases, and how these play a role in analyzing the data.
The codes and themes were produced inductively, and changed throughout
the thematic analysis. This method allows for a deeper understanding of the
participants’ responses, as we do not attempt to impose our predefined themes
onto their responses. First, we read through the transcripts in their entirety, a
fundamental step to gaining familiarity with the data [2]. Then, we generated
initial codes, which roughly grouped data points into broad categories. These
codes are listed below:
• Comments on the self-regulation activities in the app: This code contains the
info the participants told us about the app’s self-regulation activities. This
code is only covering information about the design of the activity itself (look
and feel), not the content inside it.
76
• Comments on the content in the self-regulation activities: This code contains
what the participants said about the content in our self-regulation activities.
They are separate from the activities, in that it is the actual assets inside
the activity, not the look and feel of the activity.
• Comments on the avatar: This code contains the participants’ thoughts
about the avatar. This includes: 1. What screens the avatar is in. 2.
The different avatar options. 3. The avatar’s purpose. 4. The voice of the
avatar. 5. The conversational aspect of the avatar.
• Comments on the app’s user interface (non-activity screens): This code con-
tains the participants’ thoughts about the look and feel of the app, not
including the design of the activities. This includes 1. Color, 2. Design
choices, 3. Layout of screens, and navigation in the app.
• Self-regulation activity ideas / Suggestions for our app that the participants
proposed: This code contains new ideas for self-regulation activities that the
participants had for our app. This can be 1. New ideas for new activities, 2.
New ideas to enhance the activities with new things.
• Comments about the rewards and acknowledgement screens: This code con-
tains the participants’ thoughts about 1. The reward screen, 2. The ac-
knowledgement screen, 3. The rewards collection screen 4. Comments about
the frequency of these screens showing up.
• General comments about the app: This code contains general comments
about the app. These include 1. Who they would recommend the app to
(if they would recommend it), 2. How they felt using the app, 3. Broader
picture feelings about the app.
77
• Comments on the gallery: This code contains info about the participants’
thoughts on the gallery room, and saving things to their gallery.
• Comments about the settings / trusted person / miscellaneous screens: This
code contains comments the participants had about screens that were not
captured by any of the other codes.
From these codes, we generated broader themes. From there, we could ana-
lyze the data, understanding the most salient points of each theme and gathering
interesting points.

CHAPTER 9
Discussion
After analyzing the findings of the Bodhi study, we recognize some poten-
tial improvements that could be made to Bodhi in the future that participants
expressed in the study. These potential improvements are described below.
9.1 The self-regulation activities in Bodhi may need to be more explicit
in their functionality
A few participants took issue with activities when they wanted to do something
that the activity did not have the capability for. For instance, P4 and P5 noted
that there should be an indication in the coloring activity that not all of the small
regions in the drawing are colorable. This visibly lead to frustration during the
interview, and participants both expressed surprise that they could not color those
regions. Although our original thought was that making all the small regions
colorable would be intimidating and perhaps tedious, it is understandable that
higher-functioning users (who used the activity very rapidly) would want to color
every visible region. The larger issue here however, is that the functionality of
the activity was not as users assumed it would be, which may have caused the
participants’ frustration.
Similarly, a few participants mentioned that the arcade activity should have
different games, as the participants assumed that each arcade machine would lead
to a different game. While this may just be a request for more variety in the self-
regulation activities, it is also a sign that the activities may need to be designed
in a way such that their functionality is more clear. This is a balance however, as
self-regulation is inherently an exploratory practice, and we don’t want to take the
adventurous feeling out of the activities.
100
9.2 The descriptive ability of the self-regulation activities in Bodhi
may need to improve
A few participants noted spots in the app where the language was not clear
to them. For instance, in the positive thoughts activity, P3 was confused by the
instructional text that said, “Pick one or more categories, or your favorites...”.
They argued that while they understood it after trying a couple times, it wasn’t
clear why they couldn’t select favorites and another category. Similarly, a couple
participants were caught up by the arcade activity instructions, which asked them
to “Tap on all the”, then showed the image to tap on. This may have caused
confusion because the “Tap” had a pointer finger icon next to it, but the image
they were supposed to tap on had no text complement.
We gained some valuable insight about the instructions from P4, who self-
reportedly had problems with literacy. In the video activity for example, they said
that unless the app announces the video title of the video they’re going to watch,
they may be completely surprised. This goes beyond the functionality of a screen
reader, as they also say that in the emotions activity, when one taps on an emotion
face, it should “act out” the emotion. This becomes quite difficult to implement in
practice however. aside from technical challenges, to “act out” emotions would be
assigning our notion of that emotion’s meaning and imposing that onto our users,
who may have very different understandings of that emotion.

CHAPTER 10
Conclusions and future work
Individuals with I/DD have needs and wants that drastically differ from non-
disabled folks. They also have a high likelihood of having experienced trauma
in their life. We discussed the different societal and personal barriers that may
prevent individuals with I/DD from accessing therapy for their past trauma, thus
often having to live with their untreated trauma. Individuals with I/DD are avid
users of mobile computing devices. We have shown that the prior self-regulation
apps, even those that are trauma-aware, are not suitable for the I/DD community.
Thus, making a post-trauma self-regulation (PTSR) app specifically designed for
the I/DD community is one powerful way to ensure that the I/DD community can
participate in self-regulation. Thus, we have presented the prototype and design
of Bodhi, a mobile computing app for individuals with I/DD.
We first created a preliminary prototype, using information we had gathered
from prior research with lay members and experts from our population. After an in-
formal feedback session with experts in the I/DD community, we created an initial
prototype of Bodhi, which we then evaluated with the first user study with partic-
ipants from the general I/DD community. In this study we used semi-structured
interviews with five (5) participants, with some form of I/DD, who represent the
eventual users of the app. Considering this feedback from the participants and
adopting these changes, we developed Bodhi. We evaluated the app with a second
user study, again using semi-structured interviews with five (5) individuals with
I/DD, who previously have not seen the prototype. In total, we used ten (10)
participants with I/DD for our research. Our findings demonstrated that Bodhi
is an effective app for helping members of the I/DD community engage in self-
102
regulation. The participants mostly said they would use the app frequently, and
enjoyed the different activities in the app. Below, we will outline several areas in
which Bodhi can be improved, and areas which call for further exploration. These
ideas may be transferable to other post-truama self-regulation apps for individuals
with I/DD, and many of the principles we followed may be of use to other designers
in this field.
10.1 Future work
We will mainly discuss implications of the findings of the second user study,
since the findings from the first user study were incorporated into Bodhi. These
design implications serve as actionable ways to improve Bodhi, but may be useful
to other research working on PTSR apps for individuals with I/DD in the future.
10.1.1 Exploring the avatar’s conversational ability
The avatar was universally appreciated by the participants, who applauded its
comforting nature and its customizability. We also asked some hypothetical ques-
tions about whether participants would like the ability to hold quasi-conversations
with the avatar. Some participants, such as P2, said that the avatar should ask
how the participant is feeling, then suggest an appropriate activity to do based on
the user’s response. P1 also mentioned that they would want the avatar to prompt
them more directly, asking the user things such as “What do you want to do to-
day?”. It is clear that there is more work to do in determining how participants
would like the avatar to be more interactive.
Some participants also prefer the avatar in a more limited scope that it cur-
rently appears. P5, who is very high functioning, was content with the avatar’s
current state and didn’t wish for the avatar to have conversations. They also pre-
viously noted they were not a fan of artificial intelligence-based voices, and noted
103
their negative experiences with AI-based therapy, which may have marred their
opinions about conversational avatars.
Tangential work has been done which has examined AI-based chatbots in the
context of mental health and self-regulation [1]. The application which the paper
proposes, BeCalm, targets healthcare workers, a segment of the population who
faces high levels of stress and burnout, and may experience traumatic events at
work [1]. The app was also designed specifically with a focus on confidentiality,
which allows users to potentially feel more comfortable and honest in expressing
their emotions. An approach similar to this could be adapted for Bodhi, due to its
focus on self-regulation and mental health. It would have to be adapted specifically
for individuals with I/DD, and to be trauma-aware for the I/DD community. There
is plenty to be explored here, as this app does not currently allow for different
avatar representations and customizations as our app does. This should be explored
further, as this has the potential to make the app more interactive, as long as users
who are uninterested have the ability to fully toggle the conversational ability off.
10.1.2 Exploring the user-authentication considerations in Bodhi
One important component in a self-regulation app, particularly in one in which
trauma is a factor for users and there is sensitive information, is that the infor-
mation in the app should be private. Multiple participants expressed that they
thought of the avatar as a confidential buddy, someone that they could express
their feelings to without anyone else knowing what they said. In addition, some
participants noted that they would only share some things in the gallery, as some
items are private.
There is much to be considered in protecting the user’s information in the
Bodhi app, specifically since our user base is the I/DD community. For example, it
may seem appropriate to simply password-protect the sensitive regions of the app,
104
to authenticate the intended user is the one interacting with the app. However,
this poses some problems for our community: with a shorter working memory than
non-disabled folk, how will they remember the password? How will they keep this
password private from others whom they may live with? How burdensome will it
be to enter the password every time they wish to enter a sensitive area of the app,
such as the emotions calendar?
One promising tool that may be of use is A3C, an image-association-based
user-authentication framework for people with upper-extremity impairments. [2].
Originally designed for people with physical disabilities of their upper extremities,
A3C achieves authentication through allowing users to associate personal images
(only known to them) to secondary images [2]. The key is that only the intended
user would understand the association between the images. Using image associa-
tion rather than traditional text or numerical passwords is physically easier (for
those with less dexterity), is relatively seamless with assistive technology, which
all support the selection action(eye-tracking), and decreases the cognitive load of
remembering a password and keeping it secure [2]. Evidently, many of these facets
that make it accessible to those with upper-extremity impairments also make it
more accessible to individuals with I/DD. It is reasonable to explore A3C as a
potential solution to this user-authentication question, with some adaptations.
Regardless, this question of how to do user authentication for this app needs to be
more thoroughly explored in the future.
10.1.3 Exploring a recommendation system to suggest activities to the
user
One common problem in the other self-regulation apps we explored in Chapter
2 is that despite massive amounts of content, there is no recommendation system
to suggest activities for the user. For individuals with I/DD, this can leave them
105
feeling overwhelmed and intimidated to navigate the app.
In Bodhi, we slightly remedy this by giving instructions in the activities,
and making it easy to navigate. However, the idea of a recommendation system in
Bodhi and other self-regulation apps should be explored further. Some participants
mentioned that the app should suggest them an activity to do after they tell the
app how they are feeling. A recommendation system like this could give greater
guidance to users, and give them a launching point if they are unsure which activity
they would like to start with.
The idea of personalized self-regulation activity recommendation systems have
been previously explored, in this case for people with depression [3]. In this paper,
they evaluate a machine-learning recommendation system which takes in user data,
such as which activities they interacted with after logging a certain emotion. After
a reasonable amount of user information, it can begin to recommend the user
an activity to do, based on what emotions they logged. There are also other
considerations to explore, such as how to provide more context-aware suggestions.
For example, the system should probably not recommend the user take a walk
in the park if it is past midnight. Similarly, the user’s suggested activities could
be influenced by their location, whether they are at home, or work, or another
location.
There are some common challenges with recommendation systems, such as
the cold-start problem, where the app cannot usefully recommend any activity to
the user because they have just started using the app. This is problematic because
users all the more suggestions when just starting to use the app. To remedy this,
collaborative filtering methods have been employed, which look at both the user’s
history and what other user’s experiencing similar emotions have been suggested.
There has been interesting research on how to take into account this problem, such
106
as ”Social Collaborative Filtering by Trust”, which factors in other user’s data who
the user notes they are close with and trust them [4]. To evaluate the estimated
effectiveness of certain recommendations to a user, Self-E, an app which guides
users in doing self-tracking, uses Thompson sampling to calculate the expected
reward that a certain action (in our case, being suggested and doing an activity)
has on the user [5].
In addition, we have posited in previous work that PTSR apps for people
with I/DD should ensure the principle of trust, that the user can trust the app
and feel like their information is secure [6]. There are many considerations in
how to gather data in a way which stresses informed consent, and that the user
is absolutely aware of any ways their data is used. If not done properly, the user
can lose trust in the app and not use it if they feel their information is being
utilized without their knowledge or consent. There are a multitude of interesting
and impactful directions to explore further that could greatly improve the app
experience.
10.2 The socialization aspect of Bodhi should be explored further.
We have posited in our prior work that in order to build a PTSR app for
people with I/DD, one of the guidelines to follow in doing this is collaboration [6].
That is, PTSR apps should provide a way for users to interact with other users.
This is necessary, as using a mobile-computing app, specifically a self-regulation
one, can be quite solitary [6]. Giving the ability to the user to interact with others
can decrease feelings of isolation, as users could be able to interact with family,
friends, and other people with I/DD.
There are many interesting design considerations for allowing more socializa-
tion in a PTSR app. One interesting route is pure emoji-based communication.
Recent research has shown that emoji-based communication has interesting results,
107
such as facilitating easier communication and reducing barriers to understanding
others [7]. This may be of use to people with I/DD, for two reasons: (1) peo-
ple with I/DD often prefer consuming pictorial content over written content; (2)
it reduces the amount of typing the user has to do, which reduces the cognitive
load and energy requirement. For the purposes of feeling support, while also re-
maining anonymous, emoji-based communication has shown promise as a way to
self-disclose emotions purely using emojis [8]. In this work, participants who used
Chirp (the sandbox app in the paper) reported feeling comfort by seeing other’s
emotional disclosures, which consisted of only emojis. This holds promise for PTSR
apps for people with I/DD, as it is a way for users to remain anonymous while
still feeling included and being able to semi-interact with other members of the
community. This topic of adding more socialization in Bodhi, and other PTSR
apps, should be explored further.
